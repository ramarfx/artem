Vincent Van Gogh

Paintings
The Persistence of Memory (1931)

Depicts a barren landscape with a number of surreal and dreamlike elements. In the foreground, there are several melting pocket watches draped over various objects, including a tree branch and a distorted face.

Soft Construction with Boiled Beans (1936)

The boiled beans in the title refer to a traditional Spanish dish, but here they are presented in a grotesque and unsettling manner, symbolizing the chaos and destruction of the war.

Swans Reflecting Elephants (1937)

The painting depicts three swans gliding on the surface of a lake. The reflections are the heads of elephants, which merge seamlessly with the swans to create a dreamlike and surreal image.

Metamorphosis of Narcissus (1937)

The painting depicts the Greek mythological figure of Narcissus, who is kneeling by a pool of water and gazing at his own reflection. However, his reflection is shown to be metamorphosing into a hand holding an egg, which is also being reflected in the water.

The Burning Giraffe (1937)

The painting is believed to be inspired by Sigmund Freud's theories and represents the transformation of the human psyche through fire. It is also thought to reflect the political turmoil in Spain at the time, with the burning giraffe symbolizing the destruction of traditional values and the rise of fascism.

The Face of War (1940)

A painting during World War II. It depicts a gruesome image of a disembodied head of a soldier, mouth gaping and eyes bulging, surrounded by dark and distorted shapes. Dali's intents to convey the horror and brutality of war through this painting, and it has been considered one of his most powerful.

Dream Caused by the Flight of a Bee (1944)

This represents the fleeting nature of dreams and the way they can blend reality and fantasy. The pomegranate symbolizes fertility and sensuality, while the bee symbolizes of industry and hard work.

The Temptation of St. Anthony (1946)

The painting is believed to be a representation of the struggle between good and evil, with St. Anthony representing the triumph of virtue over temptation.

The Madonna of Port Lligat (1949)

This painting represent a kind of transition for Dali, away from a complete adherence to avant-garde principles, towards a fusion of the same with more classical and religious traditions.

Christ of Saint John of the Cross (1951)

Dali's depiction of Christ with an elongated and almost weightless body, and his emphasis on the beauty of suffering and sacrifice has made the painting a powerful symbol of Christian faith.

Galatea of The Spheres (1952)

The composition of the painting is intricate and detailed, with many smaller spheres contained within larger ones, almost like a planetary system. The painting is dreamlike and otherworldly, capturing the essence of Dali's unique artistic style.

The Disintegration of the Persistence of Memory (1952)

A continuation and evolution of Dali's iconic "The Persistence of Memory" painting, featuring the same surreal, dreamlike landscape with melting clocks and distorted objects, but in a state of disintegration.

The Sacrament of the Last Supper (1955)

Depicts the biblical scene of the Last Supper, with Jesus Christ and his twelve apostles gathered around a long table, sharing a meal. However, Dali's interpretation of the scene is highly symbolic and surrealistic, with many unusual elements and distortions.
