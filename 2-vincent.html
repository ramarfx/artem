Vincent Van Gogh

Vincent
Van Gogh

Dutch painter, generally considered the greatest after Rembrandt van Rijn, and one of the greatest of the Post-Impressionists. The striking colour, emphatic brushwork, and contoured forms of his work powerfully influenced the current of Expressionism in modern art.

Vincent Van Gogh

Paintings
Le Moulin de Blute-Fin (1886)

The name of these paintings comes from one of the most famous windmills in Paris. The windmill was positioned on a raised surface and overlooked the city, and this design made it sensational.

Terrace of a Cafe on Montmartre (1886)

The painting depicts a bustling scene at a popular cafe in Montmartre, a neighborhood in Paris known for its vibrant nightlife and bohemian culture.

Imperial Fritillaries in a Copper Vase (1887)

Depicts a bouquet of golden imperial fritillaries in a copper vase, the shiny patina of which reflects the color of the flowers as the motted wall stands out with a combination colours.

View of Paris from Vincent's Room in the Rue Lepic (1887)

Van Gogh lived in an apartment in Montmartre, above the city of Paris. This afforded him a beautiful view of the Paris skyline, which he painted several times.

Italian Woman (1887)

This woman is without doubt Agostina Segatori (1843-1910), a former model of Corot, Gérôme and Manet with whom Van Gogh seems to have had a brief love affair a few months before this portrait was painted.

Sunflowers (1888)

This series of paintings depicts vases of sunflowers in various stages of bloom. Van Gogh painted a total of five versions of this scene, each with slightly different colors and arrangements.

The Sower (1888)

This painting is widely considered to be a representation of The Last Judgement in the gospel of Matthew. It speaks about how God sows the seeds of the spirit in the soil of humanity and returns on the last day to reap the harvest.

The Yellow House (1888)

The Yellow House represented a place where anything was possible. This unremarkable place became a touchstone for the imagination, something beyond mere hope, it became an affirmation that this would indeed happen.

Starry Night Over the Rhône (1888)

Van Gogh captures his characteristic magical starlit sky, peeking through the darkness of night and twinkling off the river. This respite from the darkest of days is a moment of peace, a brightness when the weight of the world feels crushing and inescapable.

The Starry Night (1889)

Van Gogh was seeking respite from depression at Saint-Rémy in southern France when he painted The Starry Night. It reflects his direct observations of his view of the countryside from his window as well as the memories and emotions this view evoked in him.

The Starry Night (2nd version) (1889)

This painting features similar elements to the earlier version, with swirling stars and a crescent moon in a deep blue sky, but has a more somber and melancholic tone.

Hospital in Saint-Remy (1889)

This painting was created during his stay at the Saint-Paul-de-Mausole asylum in Saint-Rémy, France. The painting depicts the hospital where Van Gogh was a patient for a year.

Cypresses (1889)

This painting shows a row of tall, slender cypress trees against a blue and green background. The trees are rendered in Van Gogh's distinctive swirling brushstrokes.

Irises (1889)

The irises represent the world of the mentally ill people who are in the hospital. Van Gogh is a member of that community, but he is different, he looks like a madman but he is not mad like the others, he simply suffers of epilepsy, at least that was the doctor's diagnosis.

Almond Blossoms (1890)

The almond blossom is also a symbol of bravery and courage, purity, hope, and love. From Greek mythology, the almond blossom is a symbol of eternal true love, unconquerable by death. For all accounts, the almond blossom is a sacred symbol, much like the apple blossom.

The Church at Auvers (1890)

This painting shows the church in the town of Auvers-sur-Oise, with its distinctive red roof and tall steeple. Van Gogh painted several versions of this scene, each with slightly different colors and details.

Daubigny's Garden (1890)

This painting shows the garden of Charles-François Daubigny, a fellow artist who had lived in Auvers-sur-Oise before Van Gogh. It is a vibrant and colorful scene, with flowers, trees, and a small pond.

Wheatfields with Crows (1890)

It is often claimed that this was his very last work. The menacing sky, the crows and the dead-end path are said to refer to the end of his life approaching.

Portrait of Dr. Gachet (1890)

This painting is a portrait of Dr. Paul Gachet, a physician who treated Van Gogh during his time in Auvers-sur-Oise. The portrait is notable for its vivid colors and expressive brushstrokes.

The Ravine (1890)

This painting shows a deep ravine with steep walls and a small stream running through it. The colors are rich and intense, with shades of green, blue, and purple creating a dramatic and moody atmosphere.
